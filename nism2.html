Peeer Response 1

My first peer response was for this post: https://www.my-course.co.uk/mod/hsuforum/discuss.php?d=299912

Hi Sherelle. I appreciate your post and especially some of the more novel mitigations you have suggested.

I think that direct denial of service attacks against medical devices themselves is probably an unlikely real world threat. However, attacks that result in denial of service against the controlling systems are already happening. For instance, ransomware attacks against a German hospital in 2020 are believed to have accounted for deaths as patients were turned away due to failures of the IT infrastructure (Slabodkin, 2021).

I like your suggestion of using threat detection services to provide early warning of denial of service attacks. One potentially interesting mitigation might be the use of honeypots. Honeypots are systems that mimic potentially valuable targets, so when an attack is launched against one, it can immediately alert the cyber security team. Brook (2016) describes how researchers were able to use honeypots that mimic medical devices to discover numerous attempts to exploit vulnerabilities in those devices. These results could make the case for the use of honeypots in medical environments.

Hi Ags. I think you are correct regarding your conclusions about mitigating the brute force attacks. However, I am not entirely sure if your proposed mitigation against the denial of service would be effective in this case.

You have suggested that a network firewall could be an effective control for this type of attack. I agree with you that network firewalls can mitigate certain types of denial of service attacks. However in order to perform their packet filtering functions, network firewalls need to be located on a network between a potential attacker and the target (Campbell, 2016). In the case of the iStan mannequin, the denial of service attack was conducted using the MDK3 security testing tool (Glisson, et. al., 2015). The purpose of MDK3 is to test 802.11 wireless networks (Sankar, 2018). Since the attack test took place wirelessly between the attacking system and the access point built into the medical mannequin, I do not see how a network firewall could be employed to mitigate the attack. I may be mistaken on this point, but I would love to hear if you have any further thoughts on using firewalls to mitigate wireless attacks.

Team Assignment

The team assignment this week was to prepare for seminar 2 on STRIDE and DREAD by assigning DREAD ratings to the vulnerabilities identified in the paper.

DREAD Ratings

DREAD category Brute force attack DOS attack
Damage potential 3 (attacker could gain access to the access point login details, thus getting access to the whole network) 1 (the mannequin would be taken offline impacting only the small group of people)
Reproducibility 3 (the attack can be reproduced at any time) 3 (the attack can be reproduced at any time)
Exploitability 3 (the attack can be carried out by anyone with basic computer understanding) 3 (the attack can be carried out by anyone with basic computer understanding)
Affected users 3 (after gaining access point security details, the follow up attack could impact the whole network) 1 (DOS would impact users of the particular mannequin only)
Discoverability 3 (WPS vulnerability has been known for a long time, the step by step guides are available) 3 (The TPS network protocol vulnerabilities are well known)

The resulting risk total for a brute force attack is 15 (high risk) and 11 (Medium risk). Thus, the brute force attack on a medical mannequin would have a larger impact on the medical training facility than the DOS attack. Please note, that the ‘Affected users’ DREAD category was considered to also include other users within the medical training facility network and not only those directly working with the training mannequin.

Reflections

We're into our second week, and after submitting our group contract we again had no time to waste as we needed to prepare for seminar 2 on STRIDE and DREAD. Fortunately I am very familiar with both of these concepts, having worked with STRIDE in risk assessments for years. STRIDE continues to be used out in the wild, although most practitioners tack on an additional category for "lateral movement" now when using it for network-based risk assessments as opposed to its original use for application risk assessments.

As a threat modeling methodology, STRIDE has significant problems. For one thing, the threat categories are not very intuitive -- very few people really grasp "repudiation", for instance. But the biggest drawback of STRIDE is that it provides no guidance on how to model threats other than by providing a set of categories. This means that it relies on the experience and imagination of the person using it, which can easily lead to missed threats.

DREAD is even worse. Honestly, I have never seen anyone use DREAD in practice. There are far better impact assessment methodologies now, including the popular Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS).

Our group was able to complete the preparation assignment using Google Docs for collaboration. I volunteered to present the findings on behalf of the group during the seminar.
