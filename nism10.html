Summary Post

In this collaborative discussion, we reviewed a number of case studies involving GDPR investigations undertaken by the Data Protection Commission (2020). Many of these case studies involved data subject access requests (DSARs). Ashmore (2022) and Klacar (2022) for instance both describe cases involving DSARs for surveillance video. The case study that I reviewed related to a private investigator who used contacts at the government and the police to obtain personal data on subjects without consent (Peuhkurinen, 2022). My recommendations focused on data protection controls.

One post in particular I found very interesting. Cook (2022) described how a manual process for addressing envelopes at a hospital resulted in personal data being mailed to the wrong recipient. Cook recommended that the risks involved in manual processes could be mitigated by automating the process through a secure email function, and that this solution would additionally allow for audit trails to record who accessed the data.

This was a theme that Angelides (2022) elaborated on in response to my post, suggesting that proper logging can alert when authorised employees access or share data for an unauthorised purpose. Based upon the insights from Cook and Angelides, I now conclude that my initial recommendations were incomplete and should have included the need for audit logging and log analysis. Audit log analysis can be used for both incident detection and for forensic investigations (Campbell, 2016).

Data Breach Case Study

Read Swinhoe, D., 2020. The 15 Biggest Data Breaches Of The 21St Century. [online] CSO Online.

Select one of the cases by completing Data Breach choice. Once you have made your selection, you will be able to see the links to the case. Then complete a breach checklist as discussed in the lecturecast (reproduced below):

  1. What types of data were affected?
  2. What happened?
  3. Who was responsible?
  4. Were any escalation(s) stopped - how?
  5. Was the Business Continuity Plan instigated?
  6. Was the ICO notified?
  7. Were affected individuals notified?
  8. What were the social, legal and ethical implications of the decisions made?

If you had been the ISM for the organisation you selected what mitigations would you have put in place to stop any reoccurrences?

For this case study, I chose MySpace.

What types of data were affected?

Personal data in the form of user accounts, including user names, email addresses, and passwords.

What happened?

Hackers compromised 360 million user accounts and put them up on a hacker forum called LeakedSource.com.

Who was responsible?

MySpace attributed the attack to a Russian hacker known as Peace.

Were any escalation(s) stopped - how?

I am sorry, I do not understand the question. However, the attack was limited to only user accounts on an older version of the MySpace platform, so only accounts which had been created before June 2013. Presumably the newer MySpace platform had improved security which kept the attackers from compromising new accounts.

Was the Business Continuity Plan instigated?

There is no evidence that MySpace had to execute a BCP. The attack was on an older version of the MySpace platform and appears to have not affected their production system.

Was the ICO notified?

The hack occurred in 2016, two years before GDPR came into effect. There is no reporting to indicate that the ICO was informed. MySpace did report the incident to law enforcement.

Were affected individuals notified?

MySpace notified their users via a blog post on their system. Affected user accounts also had their passwords reset so that they had to change them upon next user.

What were the social, legal and ethical implications of the decisions made?

Once the largest social media website on the planet, MySpace had declined in use significantly in the late 2000's and early 2010's. By the time this breach occurred in 2016, most news outlets treated the story as almost a joke. There is no indication that MySpace was every sued or fined as a result of the breach.

If you had been the ISM for the organisation you selected what mitigations would you have put in place to stop any reoccurrences?

MySpace appears to have already put many of the required mitigations into place by 2016 for their new platform, including proper encryption and salting of passwords. One control that should have been already implemented would be the proper decommissioning of legacy systems which are no longer in use. Older systems with known security weaknesses should be archived and taken offline.
