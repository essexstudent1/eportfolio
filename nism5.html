Hi Jurbe. Thank you for the great post. I was very interested to hear that one of your group members had a latency delay of over 400ms at hop 2. Usually the first hop in a traceroute is from the local computer to the local router, and hop 2 is the connection to the Internet Service Provider (Mitchell, 2014). This would mean that the high latency is likely a result of the Internet connection type used by this individual. According to Christiansen (2021), only satelite Internet connections tend to have latency over 400ms.

I would be interested to know if you determined the Internet connection type used by this individual, and whether you think that different connection types might affect security scans.

Internet providers often use IATA airport codes to identify the geographic location of routers on their network (Lamberti, 2020). In the tracepath, you can see that my local Internet Service Provider hands off my packets at hop 6 to Cogent Communications. Cogent clearly uses IATA codes, so you can read the path from hop 6 in Toronto (YYZ) to hop 7 in Montreal (YMQ) to hop 8 in Liverpool (LPL) to hop 9 in Amsterdam (AMS). At that point, Cogent hands the packets off to A2 Hosting for the last mile to our target server. If the packets continued back from Amsterdam to the USA, we would see additional hops after that.

It is also important to note that my longest delay occurs between 7 and 8, which is a trans-Atlantic hop from Montreal to Liverpool. Routing on the Internet is controlled by the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP). BGP uses many different factors to select a routing path, but generally speaking it tries to select the shortest, fastest, and/or most efficient route (IPXO, 2022). If the site really is in the USA, it would be illogical for my packets to go from Canada to the UK to the Netherlands, and then back across the ocean to the USA.

Metasploit

  • Easy to install (just lots of next for windows, and for kali linux it’s just a command line)
  • Ease of use - you just call the function, set destination and the host
  • Flexibility - scan, build virus, password crackers, DOS, etc. Supports many OS. High risk because it makes the machine vulnerable.
  • Licensing - open source, some libraries or sources are inbuilt and they are sold on dark web. About 20-10% of libraries, sources are charged.
  • Reputation - the tool is well known for a long time. It is good for offensive hacking/pen testing. The companies do not use it publicly as it’s a dangerous tool.
  • Privacy - Hamad thinks that it is hard to prove that the software is not sharing any of your machine data during the scans.

Nessus

  • Ease of install - easy to install.
  • Ease of use - you set up the host name/IP and scan.
  • Flexibility - extra steps to go through if you have cloud deployments. It’s more difficult to customise
  • Licensing - they charge, but they offer a trial month (with limited reports).
  • Reputation - known tool.
  • Privacy - privacy documents in place to ensure your data is secure.

Nmap

  • Ease of install - easy.
  • Ease of use - CLI based, it’s harder to use because you need to know the commands.
  • Flexibility - not customisable.
  • Licensing - free.
  • Reputation - very well know.
  • Privacy - local installation is very safe, everything is stored on your machine. Online privacy is unknown, i.e. we don’t know if they keep the reports or email it to someone - we cannot prove it is 100% confidential. Most people tend to use online version, so your IP is not exposed.

Burp

  • Easy installation
  • Ease of use - local certificate must be generated, imported into the browser you wish to use so that everything is cached on your machine. More for experienced users.
  • Licensing - free and paid version (pro and enterprise). Pro is not very expensive, but the enterprise is very pricey.
  • Privacy - the tool is installed on your machine, so as long as you keep your machine safe, the data is safe.

OWASP

  • Ease of install - easy
  • Ease of use - easy to use.
  • Flexibility - supports many OS. It is open source; developers can change the source code to suit their needs. Both manual and auto scanning allowed.
  • Privacy - plugins may be unsecure, some code could be manipulated.

Reflections

This week was the first test of whether the personality traits that I described in unit 1 would be problematic. As mentioned then, I have a tendency towards "know-it-all-ism", an irrational need to appear smarter than others. It is a particularly unflattering behaviour pattern.

The topic this week was basic networking, which was a nice refresher for me. I did a lot of network engineering in the early-to-mid 2000's, and earned the Cisco Certified Network Professional designation, before deciding to focus my career on information security.

Most of the questions in this assignment were boringly easy. However the last one - "where is the server located?" - was a bit more interesting. I had to convince them that my answer was correct -- without being obnoxious about it. This ended up being a lot harder than I expected. Finally, we wrapped up the discussion on the medical mannequin vulnerabilities this week. I found this exercise particularly enjoyable as it gave me an opportunity to do some research into real-life wireless hacking as well as some theoretical research into how consumer drones could be employed in wireless attacks.
