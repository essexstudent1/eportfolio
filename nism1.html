Network and Information Security Management - Unit 1

Objectives

  • Define the four tenets of security and understand how they underpin the field of Information Security.
  • Become familiar with the key international standards that define Information Security Management.
  • Review the two core concepts of Information Security – vulnerabilities and threats – and understand how they are related and how they can be classified and assessed.
  • Identify a range of professional roles that are available in the Information Security field.

Collaborative Learning Discussion 1 - Part 1

Read Glisson, W., Andel, T., McDonald, T., Jacobs, M., Campbell, M. & Mayr, J. (2015) Compromising a Medical Mannequin. Healthcare Information Systems and Technology (Sighealth).

Answer the following discussion questions:

  1. What are the major threats and vulnerabilities discussed in the paper?
  2. How would you mitigate against these?

You should demonstrate that you understand the topic covered and ensure you use references to academic literature (including journals, books, and reports). This activity will provide evidence of your personal growth and your summary post is required in your e-portfolio.

Initial Post

Glisson et. al. (2015) explain that cyber attacks against medical devices have been accelerating, and they describe their experiments with attempting to compromise a medical training mannequin to determine if these devices are vulnerable to attack. In this post, we will explore the threats and vulnerabilities discussed in their report and how these might be mitigated. A threat is something or someone that could cause a security incident, and a vulnerability is a control weakness or gap that could be exploited by a threat (Campbell, 2016).

In their paper, Glisson et. al. do not describe any actual threats that may wish to cause a security incident with respect to the medical mannequin. At most they mention that the FBI and other groups have released notifications about threats to medical devices, but beyond this they focus only on vulnerabilities. The two specific vulnerabilities explored in the paper are weaknesses in Wi-Fi Protected Setup (WPS) and susceptibility to denial of service attacks.

The iStan medical mannequin has its own wireless network access port which implements WPS. Viehböck (2011) explains that WPS suffers from two design flaws which makes it vulnerable to brute force attacks. Glisson et. al. used the Reaver security testing tool to successfully compromise the iStan in both physical and virtual modes using this attack method. Mitigating this vulnerability is very difficult. Viehböck recommends disabling WPS. However, WPS is implemented within the iStan’s device firmware and presumably required for the proper functioning of the device which makes it impossible to disable without re-engineering. Additionally because the iStan has its own built-in wireless access point, users are unable to implement traditional network security controls such as firewalls. The best mitigation in this case may be to keep attackers from getting within radio range of the device’s wireless access point. Reception areas, physical access and identity management, and alarms and sensors may be the most appropriate controls to secure building interiors and therefore the computing devices within (Campbell, 2016).

In addition to the WPS vulnerability, Glisson et. al. used a tool called MDK3 to demonstrate that the iStan is susceptible to denial of service attacks. MDK3 was designed to perform stress tests and penetration tests against wireless networks (Sankar, 2018). Wireless denial of service attacks are extremely difficult to prevent, and as a result the best mitigation may be to deploy a wireless intrusion detection system (TechTarget, 2009). A wireless intrusion detection system should be able to detect wireless denial of service attacks and alert cybersecurity teams who can then investigate, locate, and eradicate the threat.

Reflections

First week, and it was announced that this module we would be working in groups. This is both exciting and scary. It is exciting because I really do enjoy working with others, especially people from different backgrounds and parts of the world. It is always beneficial to learn from others’ experiences.

However it is always a bit scary as well because I am aware that I have some personality traits that can cause conflicts in group work. At the risk of sounding as though I am making excuses for myself, like many transgender people I was the target of what felt like relentless bullying in public and high school. That experience led me to develop a set of defense mechanisms, most of which have proven unhelpful as an adult. Particularly bothersome for me is a tendency towards - for lack of a better term - “know-it-all-ism”; a need to appear smarter than others. It comes from a place of deep insecurity from what I went through as a bullied, queer child and teenager. It caused problems for me the last time I was in an academic group setting, about ten years ago while doing management studies at Western University. I will need to continually check myself for this type of behavior.

Beyond that disclaimer, though, I am looking forward to the module. Our group was provided with no time to waste, and we had only a few days to agree on communication channels and get a team contract written and signed. We settled on Google Docs and Zoom to collaborate, and were able to get the contract finished on time.

We also had to jump right in to a collaborative discussion, reviewing a research paper on attacks against a medical mannequin. This is the kind of work that I enjoy most. Wireless attacks are not something that I get a lot of opportunity to research. I found it very interesting to work through the paper and do research on the attacks as well as assessing the possibilities for mitigations.
