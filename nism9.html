Hi Elizabeth. This was an interesting case study. It is hard to imagine modern organizations still hand writing envelopes, yet here we are.

Implementing security controls over manual processes is very difficult. I used to work for a large insurance company back when they would still take printouts of policy statements and hand stuff the envelopes. We had a notorious problem with "double stuffing"; putting two printouts from different customers into the same envelope.

I really appreciate your recommendation to move to a secure email system instead of mailing statements. However, moving to email does introduce a new potential risk that needs to be resolved, which is the problem of data retention and data erasion. Because Article 17 of the GDPR allows data subjects to request the erasure of their personal data, organizations must ensure that they are able to locate and delete all copies of emails which contain personal data (gdpr.eu, 2018).

This post was in response to https://www.my-course.co.uk/mod/hsuforum/discuss.php?d=306434

Hi Tebogo. This was an interesting case because the regulator in question ruled that the organization in question had acted properly, and therefore no security incident occurred (Data Protection Commission, 2017). At issue was Section 4 of the Data Protection Acts. Section 4 generally grants data subjects the right of access to their personal data, however 4(4) clarifies that data controllers should not release the personal data of third parties as part of that right to access without the third party's consent for the disclosure (Data Protection Comissioner, 2018).

In this case, there was no security incident. In order to help reduce the risk of security incidents related to access rights requests, organizations could consider implementing a Data Subject Access Request (DSAR) management tool. A DSAR management tool can help ensure that access requests are met in a timely manner while ensuring compliance with all sections of the GDPR (Ramirez, 2020).

References

Data Protection Commission. 2017. Case Studies. Available at: https://dataprotection.ie/en/pre-gdpr/case-studies#201709 [Accessed: 3 May 2022].

Data Protection Commissioner. 2018. Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003: Informal Consolidation. Available at: https://rm.coe.int/16806af232. [Accessed 3 May 2022]

Ramirez, Noah. 2020. Your complete guide to data subject access requests (DSARs). Available at: https://www.osano.com/articles/data-subject-access-requests-guide (Accessed: 3 May 2022).

Hi Ags. Thank you for the response. You are absolutely correct that the incidents identified in the case study could be considered insider threats, and that insider threat detection tools and processes should be implemented to address this risk. The main challenge with this solution is that most insider threat management programs involve significant tracking and monitoring of employee behavior. This comes with its own set of privacy issues which additionally need to be managed (Liber, A., & Bereczki, T., 2019).
