Summary Post

In groups and individually, we were asked to perform a preliminary scan of our target websites and review the results from our peers. Among the answers that we needed to find were the nameservers, mail servers, and registration information for the domains.

For the most part, these scans were straight-forward. A typical response was produced by Tolofari (2022) which correctly identified these results for the group 2 target website. The question which unexpectedly ended up being controversial was finding the physical location of the server hosting the site. Every initial post from the other groups which addressed this question placed the server in the USA, often specifically in Ann Arbor, Michigan. This is understandable as most web-based IP address geolocation sites such as geolocation.com and ip2location.com do indeed report the address as belonging to A2 Web Hosting in Ann Arbor. However, our group opted to not take this information as the final word and instead looked closer at our individual traceroutes, reviewing the hops for geographic clues and looking for who appeared to have trans-Atlantic hops and who did not. As a result we finally agreed that the most likely physical location for the server is Amsterdam, Netherlands (Angelides, 2022).

What I found most interesting about this topic was the reaction from others towards our group’s answer to this question. Rather than re-evaluating their own previous conclusions, responders challenged ours. Klacar (2022) argued that IP address geolocation should be considered reliable at least at the country level, and Jurbe (2022) suggested that because the autonomous system number (ASN) associated with the target network block is assigned to A2 Web Hosting in the USA, the site is most likely in the USA.

While this debate is something of a teapot-based tempest, I believe that it has some important lessons. Cybercriminals routinely use stealth technology such as Tor, the dark web, and overlay networks to evade detection and attribution (Campbell, 2016). To succeed against these deceptive threats, Sanders (2016) argues that curiosity, and the desire to uncover the truth beyond initial appearances, is a critical factor for success in cybersecurity.

Metasploit

  • Easy to install (just lots of next for windows, and for kali linux it’s just a command line)
  • Ease of use - you just call the function, set destination and the host
  • Flexibility - scan, build virus, password crackers, DOS, etc. Supports many OS. High risk because it makes the machine vulnerable.
  • Licensing - open source, some libraries or sources are inbuilt and they are sold on dark web. About 20-10% of libraries, sources are charged.
  • Reputation - the tool is well known for a long time. It is good for offensive hacking/pen testing. The companies do not use it publicly as it’s a dangerous tool.
  • Privacy - Hamad thinks that it is hard to prove that the software is not sharing any of your machine data during the scans.

Nessus

  • Ease of install - easy to install.
  • Ease of use - you set up the host name/IP and scan.
  • Flexibility - extra steps to go through if you have cloud deployments. It’s more difficult to customise
  • Licensing - they charge, but they offer a trial month (with limited reports).
  • Reputation - known tool.
  • Privacy - privacy documents in place to ensure your data is secure.

Nmap

  • Ease of install - easy.
  • Ease of use - CLI based, it’s harder to use because you need to know the commands.
  • Flexibility - not customisable.
  • Licensing - free.
  • Reputation - very well know.
  • Privacy - local installation is very safe, everything is stored on your machine. Online privacy is unknown, i.e. we don’t know if they keep the reports or email it to someone - we cannot prove it is 100% confidential. Most people tend to use online version, so your IP is not exposed.

Burp

  • Easy installation
  • Ease of use - local certificate must be generated, imported into the browser you wish to use so that everything is cached on your machine. More for experienced users.
  • Licensing - free and paid version (pro and enterprise). Pro is not very expensive, but the enterprise is very pricey.
  • Privacy - the tool is installed on your machine, so as long as you keep your machine safe, the data is safe.

OWASP

  • Ease of install - easy
  • Ease of use - easy to use.
  • Flexibility - supports many OS. It is open source; developers can change the source code to suit their needs. Both manual and auto scanning allowed.
  • Privacy - plugins may be unsecure, some code could be manipulated.
  • https://www.peerspot.com/products/owasp-zap-reviews

SQLmap

  • Ease of use - Linux and need to know commands.
  • Flexibility - it supports all types of databases, except non-SQL based.
  • Licensing - open source
  • Reputation - well reviewed in the IT community
  • Privacy - open source, installed locally

Kali linux

  • Ease of install - Abidemi had issues installing it on her machine
  • Ease of use - requires Linux command line use
  • Flexibility - customizable, just not easy to do.
  • Licensing - free, although some custom paid apps, but the main suite is free of charge.
  • Privacy - it can come packed with viruses, worms, etc

Jawfish

  • Ease of use - python knowledge needed for some things, they do offer free training
  • Flexibility - customisable with pyton
  • Licensing - free
  • Reputation - open source, fairly new, not as popular
  • Privacy - open source, not much more known.

Reflections

In the collaborative discussion, I started to lose my patience a bit. Our group was the only group who correctly (in my not-so-humble opinion) located the server. Yet the responses that we recieved to our post just questioned our conclusion. It brought to mind that cognitive bias where you latch on to the first bit of information you read, even if that information is wrong. And if it is wrong, it will take a mountain of evidence to convince you. You can't escape cognitive biases; they are innate. I am full of them. But you can try to lower the impact of them by realizing when you have gotten trapped by one. So I decided to make a post to that effect.

For our seminar prep this week, we had to read up on and rate penetration testing tools. I have used most of these tools at one time or another in the past, so this was a refresher for me. We also continued working on our unit 6 design document. We have many pages of raw notes, and have a skeleton for the final deliverable. We have agreed that we will all contribute 3 vulnerabilties to the document, and then we can pare them down based on word count.
