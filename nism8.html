Initial Post

For this assignment, I reviewed the case study “Prosecution of Eamon O’Mordha & Company Limited and one of Its Directors” (Data Protection Commission, 2017). Eamon O’Mordha & Company was a special investigations firm which performed work for insurance companies providing surveillance of individuals who had filed personal injury claims (Tuite, 2017). The Data Protection Commission became suspicious of how the company had come into possession of some of the personal data in the reports which they had passed on to their clients. During the course of their investigation, the Commission discovered that employees and directors of the company had used family and friends to obtain data from the Department of Social Protection and An Garda Síochána.

The offenses breached section 22 of the Data Protection Acts 1988 and 2003. Section 22 makes it illegal to obtain personal data without the approval of the data controller or data processor, and then disclose the information to another person (Data Protection Commissioner, 2008). The case was resolved when the company and the director in question both pled guilty to a number of the charges and were fined €10,000 each.

An information security manager at Eamon O’Mordha & Company Limited would have had limited means to stop this offense. It would not be considered an information security incident for a director of the company to telephone one of her nephews and request information. The information security incidents related to this case study were at the Department of Social Protection and An Garda Síochána. However an information security manager at one of these organizations could have mitigated the incident with the following controls:

  • Data classification policy. This would allow the organization to classify data according to its confidentiality, integrity, or availability requirements, and then deploy safeguards appropriate to the classification (Brook, 2018).
  • Data loss prevention (DLP). DLP technologies and processes can support a data classification policy by monitoring and blocking unauthorized transfers of data (De Groot, 2020).
  • Need to know principle. Computer systems should be configured with access controls to ensure that users are only granted access to the information that they require to carry out their authorized duties (Isms.online, ND).

Reflections

This was actually a very enjoyable week. I loved the case study assignment as part of the collaborative discussion. Researching a real world case study and applying learned knowledge to it is very satisfying. I do rather wish that the case studies were a bit more technical. My case study, for instance, involved a private investigator simply calling up her nephews and friends in the government and police and asking for favours. Even still, it was a good way to think about how security controls can interact with real world privacy breaches. I'd be happy to see more case study assignments in future modules.
